### Property Formation, Labor Repression, and State Capacity 

The creation of individual absolute ownership was an important aspect of modern statecraft. The extant scholarship postulates that local traditional elites, such as landlords or chiefs, have opposed freehold tenure and cadastral mapping out of fear of losing customary privileges and autonomy from the state. Political rulers in Western Europe had to initiate long-term campaigns to defeat local powerholders and impose these schemes. But in much of the postcolonial world, where bureaucratic capabilities are low, the support of nonruling classes to changes in land tenure becomes critical.

How do policymakers specify and allocate property rights in contexts of limited administrative capacity? And why do local elites support state-backed land tenure systems that allegedly disempower them? In my dissertation, I contend that the advent of wage labor and the erosion of slavery and related institutions---for instance, the slave trade---facilitated the introduction of absolute ownership rights. Local elites and rulers bargained for the enactment of liberal land laws that curbed elites' preexisting control of land while selectively invalidating the' *de facto* claims of free workers, thus provoking tenure insecurity, barring access to natural resources, and reducing alternatives to rural employment.
