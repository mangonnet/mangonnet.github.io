### Property Formation, Labor Repression, and State Capacity 

The creation of absolute private ownership in land was an important aspect of modern statecraft. An extant scholarship postulates that local traditional elites, such as landlords or chiefs, have opposed freehold tenure and cadastral mapping out of fear of losing customary privileges and autonomy from the state. Political rulers in Western Europe had to initiate long-term campaigns to defeat local powerholders and impose these schemes. But in much of the postcolonial world, where bureaucratic capabilities are low, the support of nonruling classes to changes in land tenure becomes critical.

How do policymakers specify and allocate property rights in contexts of limited administrative capacity? And why do local elites support state-backed land tenure systems that allegedly disempower them? In my dissertation, I contend that the erosion of forced labor (e.g., slavery or the slave trade) can prompt the enactment of liberal laws that confer individual ownership. In the absence of restrictions on the use of land, a shock to the labor repressive power of local elites in the form of abolition spurs mobility and induces labor shortages as free workers can also claim *de facto* property rights. This leads local elites and rulers to bargain for a property order that curbs elites' preexisting control of land resources while selectively invalidating the claims of free workers, thus provoking tenure insecurity, barring access to land, and reducing alternatives to rural employment. 

I test this theory using a novel dataset on land formalization, labor markets, and parliamentary voting from Imperial Brazil (1822-1889) collected during ten months of archival work. Specifically, I study how a slaveholding planter class endorsed the Land Law of 1850---Brazil's first property law---as a response to a British ban on the Atlantic slave trade. This legislation made purchase the only means by which land could be alienated and subsidized the transportation of immigrant workers to replace slave labor. First, I show that landowners in parishes with a greater proportion of slaves were more likely to comply with the Land Law by voluntarily regularizing  landholdings---old royal recognitions and informal possessions---as private freeholds. Regularization involved fiscal obligations and barriers to encroachment but facilitated the logistical resources (e.g., revenue and information on the location and boundaries of plantations) the imperial government needed to discern vacant areas, finance the arrival of immigrants, and avert squatting. Second, I show that individual parliamentarians who were slaveowners were more likely to vote in favor of the Land Law. Evidence from parliamentary minutes indicate that lawmakers who owned slaves were willing to cede the customary control of land to the imperial state to guarantee the supply of an immigrant workforce and ensure the survival of the plantation economy.
