### Property Formation, Labor Repression, and State Capacity 

The creation of absolute private ownership in land is a fundamental aspect of modern statecraft. An extant scholarship postulates that local traditional elites, such as landlords or chiefs, have historically opposed freehold tenure and cadastral mapping out of fear of losing customary privileges and autonomy from the state. Political rulers in Western Europe initiated long-term campaigns to defeat local powerholders and impose these schemes. But in much of the postcolonial world, where bureaucratic capabilities are low, the support of nonruling classes to reforms in land tenure is crucial.

How do policymakers specify property rights in contexts of limited administrative capacity? And why do local elites support state-backed land tenure systems that allegedly disempower them? In my dissertation, I contend that the erosion of forced labor relations can prompt the enactment of liberal laws that confer individual ownership. In the absence of unambiguous restrictions on land use, a shock to the labor repressive power of local elites can spur mobility and induce shortages as free workers can claim *de facto* property rights. This leads local elites and rulers to bargain for a property order that curbs elites' preexisting control of land resources while selectively invalidating the claims of free workers, thus provoking tenure insecurity and reducing alternatives to rural employment. 

I test this theory using a novel dataset on land formalization, labor markets, and parliamentary voting from Imperial Brazil (1822-1889) collected over ten months of archival work. I study how a slaveholding planter class endorsed the Land Law of 1850---Brazil's first property law---as a response to a British ban on the Atlantic slave trade. This legislation made purchase the only means by which land could be alienated and subsidized the transportation of immigrant workers to replace slaves. First, I show that landowners in parishes with a greater slave population were more likely to comply with the Land Law by voluntarily regularizing their landholdings as private freeholds. Regularization involved fiscal obligations and barriers to encroachment but delivered the logistical resources (e.g., revenue and geographic information on plantations) the imperial government needed to finance the arrival of an immigrant workforce. Second, I show that individual parliamentarians who were slaveowners were more likely to vote in favor of the Land Law. Lawmakers who owned slaves were willing to cede the control of land---based on colonial customs from the 1500s---to the imperial state to facilitate the advent of cheap wage labor.

My dissertation shows how property formation in weak states is the result of a co-production effort between local and central interests, and not of unilateral state action. Moreover, it questions the neoclassical view on the origins of private property rights. According to this literature, individual ownership presupposes scarcity. If a resource is abundant, rational agents should then forgo ownership. Conversely, I show that exclusionary property rights in countries like Brazil or Australia---where land was abundant and even free---were devised not to limit expropriation or facilitate efficient market transactions but as a political instrument to demobilize a free wokforce, skew labor markets, and ensure the survival of labor intensive agriculture.
