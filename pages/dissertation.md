A fundamental aspect of modern statecraft has been the emergence of liberal legislations introducing private absolute ownership in land. The development of the individual freehold title and the cadastral map enabled political rulers to extract tax revenue, promote markets, and gain control of the territory. An extant scholarship postulates that local traditional elites, such as landlords or chiefs, will oppose central government efforts at redefining property relations out of fear of losing customary privileges and autonomy from the state. The imposition of these schemes in Western Europe required long-term campaigns to defeat the resistance of local powerholders. But in much of the postcolonial world, where bureaucratic capabilities are low and enforcement mechanism unavailable, the support of nonruling classes to changes in the rules of land tenure is critical.
