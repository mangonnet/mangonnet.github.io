### Property Formation, Labor Repression, and State Capacity 

The creation of absolute private ownership in land was an important aspect of modern statecraft. An extant scholarship postulates that local traditional elites, such as landlords or chiefs, have opposed freehold tenure and cadastral mapping out of fear of losing customary privileges and autonomy from the state. Political rulers in Western Europe had to initiate long-term campaigns to defeat local powerholders and impose these schemes. But in much of the postcolonial world, where bureaucratic capabilities are low, the support of nonruling classes to changes in land tenure becomes critical.

How do policymakers specify and allocate property rights in contexts of limited administrative capacity? And why do local elites support state-backed land tenure systems that allegedly disempower them? In my dissertation, I contend that the erosion of forced or nonwage labor---e.g., slavery or the slave trade---can prompt the enactment of liberal laws that confer individual ownership. In the absence of restrictions on the use of land, a shock to the labor repressive power of local elites in the form of abolition spurs mobility and induces labor shortages as free workers can also claim *de facto* property rights. This leads local elites and rulers to bargain for a property order in land tht curbs elites' preexisting control of land resources while selectively invalidating the claims of free workers, thus provoking tenure insecurity, barring access to land, and reducing alternatives to rural employment.
